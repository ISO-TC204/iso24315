1.1 System overview

The Management of Electronic Traffic Regulations (METR) document series is intended to provide users access to geo-specific, trustworthy, timely, authoritative, machine-interpretable, traffic and transport related rules enacted by jurisdictional entities. 
Intro-24

1.1.1 Purpose

METR is designed to assist developers and manufacturers of driving automation systems (i.e., automation Levels 1-5) and driver information systems (including at automation Level 0) to electronically obtain traffic rules to better enable: 
Intro-25

  • interacting safely with other road users; 
Intro-26

  • following instructions from law enforcement authorities, and those authorized to direct traffic;
Intro-27

  • maintaining smooth and safe flow of traffic;
Intro-28

  • complying with other rules enacted to support legislative policies (such as environmental protection, noise, manage height and weight restrictions, and societal aspects such as market days, fiestas, pedestrian zones, etc.). [6]
Intro-23

1.1.2 Flow of information

The general flow of METR information is described as follows:
Intro-11

  • METR starts with rule-makers defining rules that are relevant to transport users;
Intro-12

  • legal rules are translated into METR rules, which is a secure, standardized electronic format that includes a digital signature of the translation agent;
Intro-13

  • METR rules are collected from various translators for a specific scope;
Intro-14

  • geo-specific rules are disseminated to transport users; 
Intro-15

  • transport-related users become aware of the METR rules, verify their authenticity, and respond appropriately; and
Intro-16

  • as needed, transport-related users can submit discrepancy reports to a discrepancy handler for investigation and correction.

1.1.4 Rule distribution

Traffic rules and their dissemination have three orthogonal characteristics that are often confused with one another.
Intro-29

  • Rules can be pre-announced (i.e., known and publicized well in advance of the user's need) or emergent (i.e., publicized and needed while previously obtained pre-announced rules are still considered valid).
Intro-30

  • Rules can be distributed through a wide-area distribution mechanism or a local distribution mechanism.
Intro-31

  • Rules can be pulled by users well in advance of their need or pushed to users as special conditions necessitate.
Intro-32

It is expected that the characteristics of users and the limitations on data capacities for local distribution mechanisms mean that virtually all permanent rules will be pre-announced and distributed from a wide-area distribution source, likely using a pull mechanism. However, any emergent rule that is activated while previously distributed pre-announced rules are still considered valid will require a push mechanism, often from a local distribution source. It is important to note that those two combinations are only typical use cases and that METR supports every possible combination of these three characteristics and addresses how discrepancies can be reported and resolved.

In addition, supporting data may provide context to the rules and can be transmitted by wide-area communication systems, roadside units, other vehicles, or on-board devices.

The rules cover virtually any rule related to surface transport systems; the graphic depicts rules for freight vehicles, kerbside usage, ride sharing, micromobility operations, vulnerable road users (VRUs), public transport usage, driving (i.e., human-in-the-loop, including driver support systems, which represent Levels 1 and 2 of automation), Automated Driving Systems (ADS, i.e., automation Levels 3-5), lane usage, public-area mobile robots (PMRs), and road works. This information needs to be available and conveyed to all transport users including nomadic devices, PMRs, and vehicles equipped with driving automation systems (i.e., Levels 1-5 of automation). Although not shown in the diagram, METR is also intended to be flexible enough to support rules relating to the use of ferries, passenger rail (e.g., trams, subways, and inter-city rail), and off-road environments.

1.3 Document overview

The purpose of this document is to define the operational concept (ConOps) for METR. A ConOps is a user-oriented document that describes system characteristics for a proposed system from the users' viewpoint. It includes a description of the stakeholders along with their roles and responsibilities. It describes what the system will do (not how it will do it) and why (rationale). The ConOps is used to communicate overall quantitative and qualitative system characteristics to the acquirer, user, supplier, and other organizational elements (ISO/IEC/IEEE 29148:2018). The ConOps is a critical tool in building consensus among stakeholders.

This document is designed to assist the following stakeholders in understanding and validating the proposed system:

1.4 Document overview

The purpose of this document is to define formal requirements for the METR System of Systems (SoS) and to introduce the reference architecture used by subsequent parts.
Intro-18

1.5 Document overview

The purpose of this document is to define formal requirements for the METR translation system, which is responsible for translating legal rules into signed electronic rules and to make these rules available to distribution systems.
Intro-19

1.6 Document overview

The purpose of this document is to define formal requirements for the METR distribution system, which is responsible for obtaining electronic rules from translation systems and distributing to user systems in an efficient and organized manner that satisfies their needs.
Intro-20

1.7 Document overview

The purpose of this document is to define the formal requirements for the METR mobile system, which is responsible for obtaining relevant rules from distribution systems and providing to the interpreter function of the METR user system that combines the rules with supporting data in a manner that fufils the needs of the ultimate user. The METR mobile system is also responsible for managing the reporting of any discrepancies discovered by the interpreter.
Intro-21

1.8 Document overview

The purpose of this document is to define the formal requirements for the METR discrepancy handling system, which is responsible for receiving discrepancy reports from METR user systems, consolidating them, and providing them to translation systems per local policies.
Intro-22

1.9 Document overview

The purpose of this document is to define the formal METR data requirements, which define the data that must be exchanged among the various METR components and between METR and external actors. 
Intro-7

This document has been developed by ISO/TC 204 in coordination with many experts from countries around the world. It is designed to be sufficiently generic to be used as an international standard applicable to any national or regional authority that wishes to adopt its processes.

System developers and system operators within authorities that adopt the METR model are advised to become familiar with this document and use it as their guide in operations.
