Vocab-32

1 Scope

The management of electronic transport regulations (METR) provides a means for METR users to obtain trustworthy, authoritative, machine-interpretable, publicly available, transport-related information for the use of the road network to better provide safe, efficient, sustainable, comfortable, and equitable transport services.

The scope of METR includes both rules that are relatively static (e.g., static speed limits) as well as those that are dynamic (e.g., variable speed limits, signalized intersections). However, where appropriate, METR incorporates existing standards (e.g., ISO/TS 19091 for signalized intersections).

This document defines terms specific to the ISO 24315 series.

3.1 Jurisdictional terms
Vocab-45

3.1.1 campus

jurisdictional area (3.1.5) with a jurisdictional entity (3.1.7) who is the owner of the area but excluding transport facilities managed by the local government
Vocab-222 EXAMPLE:

A point of interest (e.g., airport, university, business park, industrial area, shopping centre) that manages its own transport infrastructure separately from that managed by the local government. In this case, the point of interest and its own transport system are a part of the campus.
Vocab-223 EXAMPLE:

A point of interest (e.g., airport, university, business park, industrial area, shopping centre) that includes transport infrastructure that is managed by the local government. In this case, the government-managed transport infrastructure is a governmental area while the campus is represented by the remainder of the area.
Vocab-182

3.1.2 defined area

jurisdictional area (3.1.5) or sub-area (3.1.9)
Vocab-123

3.1.3 governmental area

transport-related, jurisdictional area (3.1.5) whose only jurisdictional entities (3.1.7) are governmental entities
Vocab-225 EXAMPLE:

 A subway station owned by a governmental entity is a governmental area.
Vocab-226 NOTE:

The boundaries of a jurisdictional area can include or exclude geofenced areas to indicate which jurisdictional entities have authority over transport facilities (3.4.3) as needed.
Vocab-46

3.1.4 jurisdiction

legal power to issue rules of behaviour for a defined area (3.1.2)
Vocab-227 NOTE:

An entity of a jurisdiction that has competency over a particular subject matter is called a "rule-maker".
Vocab-47

3.1.5 jurisdictional area

territory for which a jurisdictional entity (3.1.7) has the power to define rules of behaviour
Vocab-228 NOTE:

A jurisdictional area might include non-contiguous locations.
Vocab-48

3.1.6 jurisdictional boundary

territorial limit associated with a jurisdictional area (3.1.5)
Vocab-229 NOTE:

A geofence can be used to electronically represent a jurisdictional boundary.
Vocab-31

3.1.7 jurisdictional entity

person (ISO 14812, 3.1.1.6) or organization who has jurisdiction (3.1.4)
Vocab-230 EXAMPLE:

A city government
Vocab-231 NOTE:

In the case of a geopolitical jurisdiction, the jurisdictional entity is typically the recognized government for the territory; in the case of a campus, the jurisdictional entity is typically the landowner. 
Vocab-232 NOTE:

A jurisdictional entity assigns its authority to one or more rule-makers, which may be itself (e.g., the individual owner or a campus) or other divisions of the entity (e.g., police department, traffic department).
Vocab-373

3.1.8 region

Vocab-375 EXAMPLE:

European Common Market, North American Free Trade Area, Japan
Vocab-374 NOTE:

Vocab-376 NOTE:

3.3.4.1 enforcement agency

organization authorized by a jurisdictional entity (3.1.7) to enforce its rules (3.2.1.9)  
Vocab-319 EXAMPLE:

Vocab-320 NOTE:

An enforcement agency can be authorized to enforce rules for multiple jurisdictional entities. For example, the city police can be authorized to enforce national laws.
Vocab-321 NOTE:

An enforcement agency can be an independent organization or a sub-organization within a jurisdictional entity. For example, a campus can contract with a private enforcement agency or have its own enforcement agency.

A jurisdictional entity can authorize multiple enforcement agencies. For example, a national entity can authorize multiple city police agencies to enforce national laws.
